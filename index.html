Employment, Unemployment and the State of the Job Market in Australia

Introduction

This data visualisation dives deep into the trends regarding employment, unemployment and job type share Australia.

It also breaks down employment and unemployment data by state, this these values will vary across states.

Section 1: The Difference Between Labor Force Participation & Employment

The two graphs above describe the labor force participation rate and employment rate respectively

They look similar but when you hover over the lines with your mouse the values that are shown are slightly different

This is because both employed and unemployed people make up the labor force

Section 2: Unemployment Rates

This graph describes the rate of unemployment throughout the past 35 years.

It has gradually declined since the 1990s before spiking during the Great Recession and again during the COVID-19 pandemic.

After falling during the early 2020s, the unemployment rate is slowly creeping back up and is likely to spike again due to tech related layoffs.

This graph describes the percentage of women who are unemployed throughout the past 35 years.

The rate of female unemployment has actually stayed similar to the overall unemployment rate.

However, this is not the case for the rate of youth unemployment.

Although it follows similar trends to the overall unemployment rate, it is almost twice as high as the national average.

Section 3: Employment by Job Type

Although Australia is a sparsely populated country, the share of employed people working in agriculture remains very low and continues to shrink

The share of employed Australians working in the industrial sector is also declining, leaving only the services sector to have an increasing share of employment

These two pie charts illustrate the massive differences in job share from the beginning of this century to 2025.

As the share of employed Australians working in the industrial sector declines, so does the manufacturing share of total employment

Since the year 2000, Australian manufacturing has been on the decline, reaching single digit share at the end of the Great Recession, and remaining below 7% after automobile manufacturing ceased in 2017

In 2024, the share of manufacturing employment in Australia hit a record low of 6.18%, making a comeback highly unlikely, if not impossible.
