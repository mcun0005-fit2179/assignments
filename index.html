Employment, Unemployment and the State of the Job Market in Australia

Introduction

This visualisation explores employment, unemployment, and job type trends in Australia since 1990, with a breakdown by state and a comparison against the rest of the world.

Several economic events are visible in the data: the 1990s recession that peaked in 1993 with unemployment at 11%, the Global Financial Crisis that hit Australia in 2008, and the COVID-19 disruption in 2020 and subsequent recovery.

Section 1: The Difference Between Labor Force Participation & Employment

The leftmost and centremost graphs above describe the labor force participation rate and employment rate respectively. They look similar but when you hover over the lines with your mouse the values that are shown are slightly different. This is because both employed and unemployed people make up the labor force.

The rightmost graph breaks down how labor force participation changed from 1990 to 2024. The largest increase in rate of participation is from the age group of 55-64 year olds, implying an increasing average age of retirement. The only age group to record a decrease is the 15-24 year olds, inferring that fewer of them work part time jobs while in secondary or tertiary education in 2024 than in 1990.

Section 2: Unemployment Rates

The leftmost graph describes the rate of unemployment throughout the past 35 years. It has gradually declined since the 1990s before spiking during the Great Recession and again during the COVID-19 pandemic. After falling during the early 2020s, the unemployment rate is slowly creeping back up and is likely to spike again due to tech related layoffs.

The centremost the percentage of women who are unemployed throughout the past 35 years. The rate of female unemployment has actually stayed similar to the overall unemployment rate, remaining marginally above or below the overall unemployment rate.

However, this is not the case for the rate of youth unemployment, as the rightmost graph shows. Although it follows similar trends to the overall unemployment rate, it is almost twice as high as the national average, which should be concerning.

Section 3: Employment by Job Type

Although Australia is a major exporter of food products, the share of employed people working in agriculture remains very low and continues to shrink.

The share of employed Australians working in the industrial sector is also declining, leaving only the services sector to have an increasing share of employment.

These two pie charts illustrate the massive differences in job share from the beginning of this century to 2025. The services sector has grown over 6%, at the expense of the agricultural and industrial sectors.

As the share of employed Australians working in the industrial sector declines, so does the manufacturing share of total employment

Since the year 2000, Australian manufacturing has been on the decline, reaching single digit share at the end of the Great Recession, and remaining below 7% after automobile manufacturing ceased in 2017

In 2024, the share of manufacturing employment in Australia hit a record low of 6.18%, making a comeback highly unlikely, if not impossible.

Section 4: Statistics Breakdown by State

Job indicators look quite different depending on the state. NSW and Victoria have the most people, so their numbers should tend to sit close to the national average.

One indicator that was not discussed earlier in the visualisation was undermployment, which is described as working shorter hours than desired or working below one's qualifications.

As of March 2026: Australia's overall unemployment rate is at 4.3%, up 0.21% from 2025. However, there is a significant disparity of unemployment rates between states with Victoria having the highest unemployment rate at 4.7% and South Australia having the lowest unemployment rate at 3.9%.

And Finally: How Does the Unemployment Rate in Australia Compare to the Rest of the World

Although an unemployment rate around 4% affects hundreds of thousands of Australians, Australia's unemployment rate is quite low compared to some other countries. The countries with the highest unemployment rates in 2025 are Eswatini and South Africa with a whopping 34% and 32% respectively.
